# Remember Me in the Meat

- **作者**: Sarah Pauling
- **来源**: Clarkesworld Magazine, Issue 233, February 2026
- **字数**: ~12000+ (全文较长，此处为抓取到的部分)
- **原文链接**: https://clarkesworldmagazine.com/pauling_02_26

I know you don't remember me. Last night, your cult erased me from the memory of all mankind.

Each sister disciple gave me a parting gift, knowing she'd forget me on the hour. From Abbie, a vintage deck of cards. From Liv, a piece of petrified ginkgo, polished to shine.

"A reminder," she'd said, voice nearly lost to the server fans of the inner sanctum, "of Mother Gaia's resilience. Of why we fight."

Liv gets off on making grave pronouncements nearly as much as you do. I took the trinket with all the solemnity expected from one of your memory-martyrs. The others, sobbing, pressed things into my hands: baubles they saved when they left home, left the grid—as much as we could leave, with the grid living in our heads. The best we could do was obey your call to squat in an illicit bunker-cum-server-farm thirty miles outside Seattle.

Religions have been born from less.

Your own gift was an autoinjector's worth of neurotoxin.

"It's your knife," you told me. Your tattoos changed color with your body's pH; carnations crowned your brow. "Can burn through a Mindmesh and the lobemeat behind it in seconds. I chose you for this, Iris. Make the bitch pay."

It wasn't the kind of gift I'd wanted from you—secretly, softly—but I'd always known you to take more than you give. I bowed my head, surrounded by blinking server lights inscrutable as runes. It was hot enough in there to make me want to kill someone.

"I erase you," you mouthed, pushing me into a hard folding chair.

"I erase you," my holy sisters said, louder. "I erase you!"

You jacked into your network, cables snaking from your wrists like stigmata.

I'll confess something strange: as you looked down on me with godlike pity, I imagined writing you into bio-memory. Storing you in lobemeat, down to my viscera.

Pervy of me, I know. Nobody takes up brain space that way anymore. Nobody needs to, with the Mesh inside us all-consuming and all-connecting: every memory uploaded to the cloud in an instant, high-res. I can only explain the fantasy as a kind of grief.

One second, two. Then you collapsed at my feet. You lay your cheek on my knee, fiber-optic cabling consecrating the ground around us.

"Become a ghost," you said sluggishly, already exhausted. The servers whirred with your effort, louder than the sisters' chanting. "Become a knife. Save us all."

The sisters' eyes rolled back as they cleared their local caches manually. By the time they woke up—by the time you woke up—I'd be gone, and all you'd remember was sending someone to kill Clara Amsel. The who of it wouldn't matter anymore.

You drooled on my leg. Then you did what you always do—what you've done for other saints before me—and erased my ID from the Mesh network entirely. A worm of global scale, propagating. Your specialty; your life's work. The thing they'd kill you for, if they knew.

And the whole damn world forgot about me. Because the Mesh is the world, more or less.

You were wrong that I'd keep all my memories. Don't make that face; sometimes you're wrong.

Your zero-out command was indiscriminate. Every scrap of me had to vanish from the cloud, no matter whose engrams I was stored in, or anonymity wouldn't take. My own uploads weren't immune.

I mean, yes, I remember who I am. My cache retained a good chunk of me, since you were so kind as to make my local copy authoritative. I have childhood memories stored in lobemeat, too, from before the Mesh install—but they're fuzzy, and accessing them gives me a bitch of a synchronization migraine. Mesh memories are a thousand times more perfect. No gaps, no distortions. Available to view on demand.

I remember my mom, my prom, several partners I'd rather forget. I remember watching roller derbies from warehouse floors and then, later, Sounders games from private suites.

I remember all the terrible shit that happened to me. I remember the white-hot urgency of finding you; the why.

But there's data missing now: anything I didn't cache is irrecoverable. My cousin's face, modeling jobs, camping trips—hell, an entire year is missing from when the worst of the depression hit. Good fucking riddance.

Our brains adapt. The unimportant bits atrophy. People harp on about the bards memorizing Beowulf; I'd rather use my lobemeat for living. Procedural memory can stay, and I'll exile the rest to a cloud that remembers names and faces better than I ever could. I don't mind. My me is only half-meat.

It's the human way.

Anyway. Killing Clara Amsel.

I left the bunker before anyone came back online. The hike down to Hobart was night-cool against my skin. I had the cash cards Abbie'd prepped for me, and a couple more I took from your wallet while you slept. You did a good job with the car: tucked away on a trailhead with false plates and registry.

I had maybe twenty hours before Clara's corp injected untested aerosols into the stratosphere against the advice of any climate scientist not already on her payroll. The definition of billionaire Dunning-Kruger, ready to fuck up the carbon cycle on a hope and a prayer. She'd moved the timeline up twice already; you'd told me to haul ass.

I kept your neurotoxin and Liv's petrified ginkgo together in my flesh pocket.

You bought me that. It's a little compartment on my thigh, hard to see from the outside. Not the only mod you prepped me with, but it might just be my favorite.

I'm a private person, you understand.

Two hours to Anacortes and the ferry. The car routed through Kirkland, where a police cam pinged my Mesh for identification and then—within seconds—got whammied by your worm again and canceled the request. It couldn't hold on to the concept of me.

Fucking surreal.

I tried scanning the news to keep me grounded, but that just made me giddier. Suddenly, I could access Mesh data without tracking software hitching a ride. Sponsors and spyware slid right off me. I beat my hands on the dashboard in a fit of feral joy.

You picked me for martyrdom after I'd bested my sisters in endurance, in rhetoric, in unarmed brawls. One time, you stranded me on the mountain with a bag over my head and dogs at my heels. You probably forgot that night along with the rest of me.

Sorry. The dogs weren't so bad; Sonia and Didi treated the bites. What I mean is: you wanted us to know that your worm was a painful necessity. You said your brutality would only be a fraction of the pain of losing everyone we'd ever loved.

I wanted you to pick me, so I talked about memory-martyrdom as a grave sacrifice.

I didn't tell you how badly I wanted to be a ghost.

There are other things you didn't understand about me. But we'll come to that.

You told me to avoid interactions with noncombatants—that's what you called them, "noncombatants." Guess that's the dichotomy when you see yourself as a god's general-in-chief.

I should tell you I slipped up on the ferry. But nobody got hurt; not really.

I caught the last slow boat out of Anacortes. Ultralight skimmers would be running all night, bringing Puget Sound billionaires home in ones and twos from their Seattle benders. But the wealthy islanders have to get working stiffs to their shores somehow—who else would make their cocktails and clean their toilets?—and a century-old WSDOT ferry still fits the bill.

My route to Clara would be straight through one of those poor fuckers once I disembarked. Robbie Yu, a rent-a-cop from her estate, hadn't rotated the encryption on his Mesh uploads. You read his memory like a book last week.

I sat in a window booth, watching for herons in the dark. Strangers used to look at me with interest, but lately their eyes slide right past, even without the worm. I look different: too skinny under my skinny jeans, hair unkempt.

A couple came down the walkway, nuzzling each other, pitching as the boat banked. They seemed drunk.

One of them was the boyfriend I'd had at nineteen.

Crazy, right? I hadn't seen him since college, and now here he was on my way to kill a billionaire. One of life's little miracles.

They ducked into the galley. Without thinking, I stood up to follow. I tried to smooth down my hair—I used to model; used to be pretty enough to catch some big fish—then gave up and put it back with the claw clip one of the sisters gave me.

He still had that cute freckle on the back of his neck. His girlfriend was choosing a corn dog across the room, so I bumped my shoulder against his at the coffee counter. "I'm sorry, but you look really familiar. My name's Iris. Do we know each other?"

His good-humored grin smacked me open-palmed with nostalgia. "I hope so. Did you go to UW? I was on the solar sail team that won nationals. Sometimes people remember my face."

"I don't think that's it."

"Oh, hmm, well—"

"Don't worry about it." A buzzing climbed up past my fingers and into the lines of my palms. "If you don't remember me, we never met."

"Sorry," he said sweetly. "Probably not."

"No problem," I said just as sweetly. "You're right, by the way: if the cancer hadn't killed your mom, the postpartum would've. She wished she never had you."

He went white. Like the part of him that wanted to hit me hadn't quite woken up yet. That's how it went when we were together, too. Gave me a chance to leave his apartment before he started throwing things.

"Benji?" his girlfriend said. She looked maybe a decade younger. I could've warned her about him, but she would've forgotten anyway. I left the galley.

You know what I felt, then? Joy. Blunt as a baseball bat.

Not some weepy shit, like that I realized he couldn't touch me anymore. I knew that already; I'd replaced him with other abusive shit-smears in the many years since. Been there, done that, got the concussion.

It was more like validation. An honest version of him would have always reacted to me that way: indifference. I had made such a small footprint in his life, despite the way he'd cratered mine. Your worm just made it literal.

See? I gloated to an invisible jury. See? I told you. I wasn't anything to him.

The sheer fucking joy of being nothing. I got hooked on it then and there.

Sometimes I think I am more petrified than Liv's ginkgo. Sometimes I think I was already a ghost.
