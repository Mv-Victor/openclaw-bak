FIRST FORTUNE: CLADOMANCY

There are a thousand thousand routes to divination.

I say routes because that is what they are; that is what my grandmother first taught me. We stand before an infinity of paths: they branch and jostle and compete and finally converge at a single destination.

Some routes are easier and some are clearer. Some expose summits other routes conceal. Each, however, leads us to the same end.

Every part of every planet is speaking to us. Each trailing meteor, each blade of grass—all of it calling out the steps to a single universal dance.

We have forgotten how to listen. We stumble through our lives the best we can, caught on paths we cannot see, held by orbits we cannot detect.

The future can be found in a desert or a grain of sand. Precious few are left who remember how to read it.

There is dendromancy, the prophecy of trees. There is lithomancy, the oracle of ancient stones. There is even aleuromancy, one of my own favorites, which finds the future in a selection of small and rather delicious cakes.

The methods and the titles of divination come naturally to me. Across the meadowlands of Alcestis, there is no one who knows them better. The paths were opened to my family a long, long time ago. We have been the seers of this world ever since.

We have counseled shepherds and directed the planting of crops. We have provided cartomancy in the drawing up of fresh townships, and we have accredited true love when runaways meet by night beside the banks of the river Sur. We are esteemed, and we are beloved, and through it all, we hold one truth close.

We have forgotten how to listen, too.

The paths closed generations back, in the time of my grandmother's grandmother. Even then, I have my doubts. What reason did we have to hold the gift of foresight? What genetic quirk attuned us to the language of the universe? If ever such a spark existed, it is long since died out.

I would ask my parents, my ancestors, but I cannot. I am the final fragment of our once-great house.

So, then: I am a fraud. I count cards, I work tricks. I have a bag of dusts and potions and a thousand thousand ways to spy a future that I cannot see.

The effect is more or less the same. Sham prophets have existed since before the first nomads settled on Alcestis, since before Alcestis was sworn to the Ducal Fold and incorporated, shaggy and uninhabited as it was, into the grandeur of that vast domain.

I cannot read a desert, and I cannot read sand, but I can read people and tell them words they will believe. The rest is theater, the rest is presentation.

This being so, I was not overly concerned when soldiers came and crashed themselves through the low stone of my doorway. The Ducal crest shone out in scarlet tone against the beaten metal of their helmets.

I gathered up my bag of tricks and followed them without delay, out past the river, past the community that I had cherished and I had deceived, toward the hulking, gunmetal imposition that was the duke's official residence.

I had spent my life lying. The sun caught itself in the tight folds of the river Sur, and I smiled politely to the soldier beside me and thought that lying to nobility would be very much the same.

The Ducal Embassy squatted over a pleat of hills that overlooked the river town.

The base of the embassy was Alcestin in design: thickly dovetailed wood, a forest reassembled. Rough to the touch, unvarnished. Wide-windowed and functional, a compromise with the land.

The drop-ship had come the week before, in a halo of wind and storm. It had lowered itself down, an almighty gray carbuncle, and docked itself along the wooden seams of the embassy's peak. Something metal and alive had laced the two of them together, had turned a low Alcestin barn into something like a fortress.

I climbed a stairway that had once led to a flattened roof. Now there was a doorway of reinforced metal, a variation in the pressure of the air.

I stepped into another world, and the rush of heat almost bent me double. Hot air, humid air. I felt the charcoal 'round my eyes begin to run.

It was nighttime, here. A domed sky, metal arcing overhead, and beyond it the pitch and glimmer of vacuum. A colossal planet above it all, greens and blues corrupted with the grays and yellows that denoted civilization.

Somewhere beyond me, a thin voice.

"The speed of light, of course. A matter of minutes now, I should think. We are so very far away, you see . . . "

The dome enclosed something a little like a greenhouse and a little like a library. Carefully rooted rainforest erupted all around, a whirl of dripping lianas and sweaty branches and wide-brimmed crimson blooms.

He was pacing up and down a narrow clearing. Behind him was a long table, and on it lay haphazard tools, a variety of stringed instruments in disrepair.

Duke Ernestid Arkady, protector of the Fold, and ruler of every world I might hope to see. He beamed and beckoned my approach.

"Ah, soothsayer!" he said. "Welcome to my Aviary."

"Prosperity, my lord!" I cried and prostrated myself at his feet. "Prosperity for all! Long life and prosperity!"

"Well," he said to someone who wasn't there, "wasn't she quick!"

The duke smiled again. "I've come all this way to see you, soothsayer," he said. "I think we can do a little better than that."

"What reading would you prefer, my lord?" I asked. "Will it be the cards? Or haruspicy?"

I looked evilly toward the row of birds. They fluffed themselves, as if in determination to keep their guts on the inside.

"Tea leaves," he said, still fiddling with the instrument.

"No," he said, and the frailty of his voice altogether drained away. "People used to read the future in tea leaves. But they were small. Small people, small lives."

"My future, soothsayer, is something rather larger."

"Cut the mummery," he said. "You're an intelligent woman. I'm here looking for the truth, you understand me? The truth is what I intend to get."

"You might not like it," I said.

"I am dying," he said. "Tedious, but there it is. Flesh fails. Nothing that can be done."

"You want to know if the Fold will endure? The succession?"

"I wish to see posterity. I need to know how I am remembered."

"Beyond subjects. Beyond empire. At the end of it all. At the death of the universe. Does love reach that far, soothsayer? Does anything? Tell me. My name, my acts. Does any of it still endure?"

"I want you to read my future in a shattered planet," he said. "In the entrails of an entire civilization."

I looked up at the glittering world above me and searched for words of reason.

"No," I said. "All paths lead to the same destination. There's really no need—"

"Cladomancy," he said. "The reading of the future in a planetary disaster."

"It's an inhabited world," I said.

He shrugged. "They've been given due notice. I'm told there was some limited evacuation."

"I won't do it," I said. "Find someone else."

"I'm a fraud, you see. I always have been."

Something tight within me unwound and disappeared, like a child's balloon.

Above us, silent, Kalliston crumpled and burst along its seams. Long strings of continent unwound like peel, like calligraphy bleeding out into space. There was volcanism, and there was a shock of liquid boiling away, and as the detonations ceased, the fragments drifted by and made such pretty patterns as they went.

Murdered Kalliston, screaming out a message that I could not hear.

The duke smiled, and his future was written across the entirety of the sky.

SECOND FORTUNE: BELLOMANCY

The snow-white parrots of Duke Ernestid wheeled their way above the battlefield.

The Embassy had been razed to the ground, drop-ship and Alcestin timber both. Only its lowest layer had been preserved, the stony cells that drilled down beneath the earth.

War had come to the vastness of the Fold, as its ruler had known it would.

Now the war was done. The rage of it was all burned out; the rage and the tyrant both.

I picked my way in the direction that had been the river town.

The woman in the rubble was not Alcestin. Her hair was cropped and dark. Her combat uniform was blackened, made sticky by the thickness of her blood.

"Hello," I said.

"Hello," she said with unexpected vigor. "Have you come to kill me?"

"No," I said. "Just for the company."

"Hanne."

"It's my name."

"Sorry," I said. "Hanne. I've never done this before."

"It's easy," she said. "All you have to do is wait."

"I'm Baeliss," I said. "I live here."

"Must be nice," she said. "Alcestis. You've got those . . . those . . . "

"Al-gulaal," I said. "We call them al-gulaal. The things with the horns."

"I saw one. In a zoo, once."

"You don't know them," I said. "They're all pricks."

"Yeah," said Hanne. "That's what I like about them."

"Where are you from, Hanne?"

"Kalliston," she said. "He's dead, at least. I think that means I won."

"I'm not lying," I said. "I really think you're getting out of this."

When I turned back, Hanne was shivering.

"Hanne," I said, "I didn't mention something earlier. Something important, really."

"My name is Baeliss Shudal, and I'm, I'm a fortune teller."

The old lie blazed. It was heat, and it was fury, and this time, it was going to get her out alive.

"I'd very much like to read your fortune. Your lifeline is exceptional."

"Bellomancy," I said. "Tells your future through the positioning of a battlefield."

"The first thing is, you're going to live. There's a place up ahead where three tracks cross, where the treads from artillery have made a sort of star. And that means that you are fine."

"And there's more. You'll have a big house, and you're going to have a child!"

"Who with?" she asked. "Tall and handsome?"

"No," I said, "short and off-putting. I don't make the rules."

The medics came, and I sat with Hanne while they worked.

THIRD FORTUNE: OSTEOMANCY

Years passed before I told another fortune. Alcestis was in recovery.

I had woken that morning suffused with strange purpose. Instead of drinking my fermenting bucket-brew, I found myself walking to the far hills.

"Hanne," I said, and the universe around me sighed with relief. "Something told me you'd be here."

Hanne lived in a block of temporary flats. "It's not a big house," she said. "But I'll let you off with that one."

Hanne's baby pawed at my velvet pouch with some fascination.

"I want to read her fortune," I said.

"Osteomancy," I said to Braela, "tells your future through the positioning of bones."

I shook out the midnight little pouch, and the bones of Duke Ernestid's hand shattered themselves across the breadth of polished wood.

I spent my childhood staring into pools of milky bones, and they never spoke a word. They were random, and they were chaos, and I was left to build a future the best I could.

I sat in Hanne's living room and bounced Braela on my knee, and this time the bones on the table were an open book, written in a familiar hand, and everything that she would live and love and do was very plain to see.

I found a quiet place and watched the water work its course. The pattern of it was endless. Darkness, and light, and shadow, and reflection.

I skimmed the finger-bones out onto the water and watched them sink away into the glossy and infinite black.
