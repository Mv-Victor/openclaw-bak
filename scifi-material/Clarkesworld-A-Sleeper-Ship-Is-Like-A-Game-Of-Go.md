# A Sleeper Ship Is Like a Game of Go

- **作者**: Claire Jia-Wen

I can see everything. Everything that will happen, some of what has. I won't see until you ask, though.

So, ask.

This is the story of how it will begin.

You'll step from the Diviner first. I'll follow. All the other passengers will still be on shaky legs, but you and I will be steady enough to emerge from the ship and explore our new home.

The Diviner will have been calibrated to match the gravity of the planet, but we'll still marvel at the simple delight of flat ground, gravel crunching beneath our feet, substantial in a way the gray tiles of the ship could never be. We're five months before you remark to me that the moons look like the last peanut shells that nobody wants to crack, and I'll never be able to look up at the violet sky without thinking about my half-eaten bag of them sitting on my shelf back on Earth. But in that moment, you've never drawn any line between peanuts and moons, and I'll think: this is somewhere I don't mind living for the rest of my life.

The Laurabots will have finished building the city for us before we arrive. It will have been modeled after San Francisco: densely packed skyscrapers at the north end, a bridge to nowhere that's vaguely evocative of the Golden Gate Bridge beside it, the terraformed Twin Peaks rising from the center, all of it rimmed with greenhouse domes.

"Well, it's big enough," I'll say.

"It's fucking massive," you'll reply, because you could never resist an easy opportunity to contradict me. You'll have removed your helmet, and the wind will be ruffling your already-tousled hair into a good approximation of a bird's nest.

"We don't have to see each other again, you know. So this might be goodbye."

"We still have orientation," you'll say, grimacing, and I'll discover that I find your chipped incisor incredibly endearing. "I don't want to do that thing where you say goodbye and end up walking in the same direction so you have to awkwardly say goodbye again."

But tell me about the past, you say. Tell me about how it all started.

Why? I ask.

There's something I'm trying to see.

This is the story of how it began.

Morgan Wilson sat at the corner table of the Spaceway lunchroom and watched her colleagues pile their plates with glistening salads from the bar. They handed takeout containers to the four-armed robochefs and got back boxes bulging with Mongolian BBQ or lobster rolls or Chick-fil-A sandwiches. Some filled multiple containers. Probably to bring back to their families, poor bastards.

Morgan's lone container was her only company at her table in the corner. She never sat with the others because they worked on exciting stuff like the specs for the next tourist liner to Mars and she worked on dumb stuff like deciding what data went where. When people looked like they were considering the slimmest possibility of sitting with her, either out of pity or friendlessness, she made eye contact with them and held it for an uncomfortable amount of time, until they thought better of it and turned away. Her quarterly performance reviews consistently said she needed to put more effort into being a team player.

It wasn't terrible, sitting alone. She could overhear delightful snippets of who was gunning for a transfer to the New York office and who was in the incestuous polycule beginning to develop in that one high-security project team.

Everybody noticeably stiffened, conversations halting and even the lights seeming to shine more harshly, when the boy who would one day become me wandered into the room. He was a shock of dark hair, a head too big for his body, and wore expensive sneakers he'd surely grow out of by the end of the year. Rembrandt Crow's son.

Rembrandt—Remmy, as he liked for everybody to call him—was the Spaceway CEO and had been given to bringing his youngest son around following the recent news that the CEO of Epilog had disowned his own son for meeting with an anti-space colonization league.

The kid stopped at Morgan's table. She had been dreading he would.

"I'm going to sit here," he said, as if she should be grateful he gave her the attention.

But where others might have seen a spoiled brat, Morgan saw shining opportunity. She had been petitioning for the robochefs to get the license to make Krispy Kreme donuts for months.

He said, "Do you know how to play Go?"

I won't see you for a while. Our suites will be on opposite ends of the city; I'll be living above the Brazilian steakhouse, between a guy who wears exclusively black puffer vests and an old woman who likes to phone home at three in the morning, and I won't have asked where you were assigned.

It'll be an adjustment for me. Ours will be a city of small miracles, roaming wherever I please without having to ask for permission to leave my residence, coming home to find that the Laurabots fluffed my pillows, the microwave has been wiped clean, there's a media library that holds every show and film I could ever want to watch.

Every morning, a legion of bots—not bipedal with a face-screen to convey emotions like the Laurabots—but lesser bots, with exposed metal and humming treads, will disappear into the foliage surrounding the city and return every evening with bellies full with water to be run through the sanitation plants gurgling below our city, bearing live specimens and mineral samples to be analyzed, the results sent back to Earth.

The bar where I'll finally meet you again will be a few blocks from Not Golden Gate Park. It'll be my favorite because there will be a popcorn machine and the people won't be as insufferable as that one place in the Not Marina—you'll know the one.

At some point in the night, we'll converge at the bar. The latest episode of Killer Mechs will be playing on the holo above the counter. I'll be half-heartedly shooting the shit with the Laurabot bartender, who will have correctly assessed my true motive of maximizing the time I spend away from my date. You'll order seven AMFs.

"You don't think they're annoying?" I'll nod at your friends, who are screaming as they take a group shot like no group of people has ever taken a shot at the same time before.

"Do you think Mr. Quarter Zip is annoying?"

"Yes. He's decent at oral, though."

You'll furrow your brows. "I guess I do. Find them annoying, I mean. They find me annoying too. But I'm used to being surrounded by those kinds of people."

"Do you want to get out of here?" I'll say.

As they set their pieces upon the board, Morgan learned that the boy's name was Maxexcelson, shortened to Mason, and that he was marginally more tolerable than her colleagues. Morgan had never played Go, but she knew the rules because her professors used the game as a toy example in reinforcement learning and explore/exploit. You needed five in a row to win, in any direction.

"My dad says there's no point in playing Go or chess anymore," Mason said, setting a white piece to form a row of three.

"And why is that?"

"He says there's no point. AugurMind and Big Blue can already beat the best humans at it."

"What are your favorite foods?" Morgan said, setting a piece and hoping Mason wouldn't notice she could get four in a row the next turn.

He blocked it. "I like katsu curry, but only if it's non-synth. My dad gets this place droned to us every month."

"Anything sweet?"

"Some of the employees give me the candy they get from their Halloween parties."

"What about pastries or baked goods?"

"I mean, they're okay. My dad doesn't get them too often because he says they're a waste of time."

"Have you ever had Krispy Kreme?"

"I haven't left here in a long while. The me my dad carries around on TV is a body double. He says I'm too important to be taken out of here."

Morgan spent so much cognitive effort parsing the implications of his statement that she didn't notice he'd set up a runaway four in a row.

"Morgan," Mason said, one day. Crusts of frosting lined his mouth. "How do AugurMind and Big Blue predict the future?"

Eventually, she said, "There are more states of chess than there are atoms in the universe, and there are more states of Go than there are atoms in the universe squared. So you can't look at every possible way a game might happen, you don't have the time or the energy. You just look at the most likely ones."

"So they don't predict the future?"

"No. They look at the probable futures and select the most promising next move. But they can't decide what the other player puts down. That's up to them."

Mason didn't reset the game. He stared at her with so much concentration and for so long she wondered if she also had frosting on her face. "You were wearing a green shirt yesterday, right?" he said. "And you had matching eyeshadow. You tied your hair into a ponytail. Your shoes had buckles. We talked about how San Francisco used to be way foggier, how sugar gets overly villainized by the guys in the news, and how I probably shouldn't watch Enchanted until I'm older, in that order."

All those things were true.

Frowning, Mason massaged his temple.

I'll ask you if you've been to the Not Golden Gate Bridge yet. You'll say yes, but you're happy to go if I haven't; I'll admit I haven't, and tell you that you don't need to remind me it's getting less and less socially acceptable not to visit the biggest landmark we have.

What a strange sight it'll be, a bridge over nothing and leading to nowhere, rusted red extending beneath a bruised purple sky. Four-winged avian creatures will settle along the drooping cables, their keening calls spearing into the night.

We'll walk along the asphalt, casting long shadows where the cars would usually be. The lane dividers will gleam up at us like new dentures—they'll never be dirtied by tire marks or cracked by water damage. Just our footprints.

I'll tell you about Galileo who was protected by the de Medici Clan, Galileo who discovered the moons of Jupiter and gifted them to Cosimo II and named them the Medicean stars. As if you could claim a string of stars merely by reaching out and closing your fist around them!

You'll trace a path between a few with your finger, like you're playing connect the dots, and say, "That one's yours, then. I'm calling it the Hip of Theseus. For you."

"I'm Theseus now?"

"What else do you call a hero who steals the beleaguered princess away from the terrible monsters at the bar?"

But you're no Ariadne, and I'm no Theseus.

I'll draw a bear between five stars. "That one will be Contrarian Major then."

You'll open your mouth, close it, wanting to prove me wrong, not wanting to prove me right. I'll laugh and name another constellation something more flattering. You'll remark that the moons look like peanuts.

Then Rembrandt announced the Diviner project.

For the meager price of a few million dollars, anybody could hop on a spaceship and join the first exoplanet settlement. And this wasn't like some grungy sci-fi-style mining project. This was like glamping for space colonization. Passengers aboard the Diviner would remain suspended in cutting-edge sleeper cells, and the hibernation fluid would ensure they stepped off the three-decade journey no older than when they stepped on. The newly unveiled Laurabots would build the city and cater to the inhabitants' every need.

He was sending one of his sons on the journey, as well.
