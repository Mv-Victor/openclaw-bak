# Chip

I didn't have that cliched cloth bundle tied to a stick that peregrinators carry, but I certainly felt like one. Instead of a cloth bundle, it was the holopack I salvaged from the dumpster that I slung over my shoulder as I hailed a robocab. I waved, but the fancy robocabs ignored me as they scanned for signs of wealth. I had none. The torn jinbei with its half sleeves I fixed up with the help of a sewing bot from the used shop would not impress any robocabber.

I'd rebooted the holopack and given it upgrades that I'd illicitly downloaded. One of the upgrades allowed it to enumerate all my contents to me, telling me which pockets everything was in. I liked a certain order to my chaos.

"Lí-hó, Bao bao," I said, invoking and greeting my holopack. The last user was from Taiwan, and I found that the AI responded better to my poor attempts at Taiwanese Minnan. My parents spoke it, but I could only manage a few words. Just as people change personalities in different languages, it turns out so do AI. Maybe the pack was made in Taiwan. Who knows, since the tag fell off by the time I scavenged it.

"Gâu-tsá! Lí-hó, Mx. 夢香 Mengxiang," said the holopack. It had an eager lilt to its voice. The way it said my name was very affected, drawing out the vowels, mEng xIAng, as if emphasizing its etymology. Like my name, I really did dream to be aromatic. I had a sharp sense of smell after all. Though now, I was more likely redolent of sweat and BO after my recent escapades. "Tsia̍h-pá—buē?" it continued.

Hm, have I eaten yet? I thought, as my stomach growled. "Guá tsia̍h-pá—ah," I said, lying. I was nowhere near full. I didn't want the holopack to offer any snacks. The last thing I needed was a brittle, expired sesame seed bar hanging from my lips. That about exhausted my Taiwanese language knowledge.

"Wipes please," I asked in English.

It flashed a blueprint of its innards and an arrow to the location.

"Inner pocket, Sì A, 4A," it said. Immediately, it became less warm and more technical and efficient when I switched from what I imagined to be its native Taiwanese setting to a translated English setting. Though the pack still threw in a few words in Taiwanese Minnan here and there for effect.

"Thank you, kám-siā," I said, responding in linguistic kind. I grabbed the wipe and rubbed myself down for good measure. The green tea-infused wipe left me smelling clean and perked up my senses. "Can you find the applicator for a cosmetic overlay?"

The overlay wouldn't fool a fancy robocab, like one of the Automatikwagen Dragonflies with their sleek iridescent latticework detailing that sparkles in the sun, but it might catch one of the old Afford Trolls.

"Side pocket, cha̍p-jī C, 12c," said the holopack. The view of the holo blueprint shifted, and the arrow traveled alongside it to provide me the best vantage point to view the locator map. It was like digging for treasure. I finally got out the applicator.

I ran it down my skin from a few centimeters away, making sure I got my short black locks. I put it away, with the holopack giving me a gentle haptic shock to let me know it was snug in place. I calmed my nerves, counting to ten in Taiwanese. This was my first day here in the city. I was hoping things went well and I could land a job. My plan was to visit the recruiter this coming week, but since today was a free day, I wanted to walk around and see the botanical garden, hawker row, and the cloudpiercers.

One clunky made-for-efficiency Afford Troll stopped for me, as well as a refurbished jeepney. A-ha, caught one! I stepped into the Afford Troll, not feeling like sharing a ride and wanting something more straightforward. The jeepneys were made for multiples and often included spectacle entertainment, like mini drone light shows. I needed a break.

No unfurling of a red mat followed like some of the fancier cars. Instead, it projected a red carpet, which shone on my skin, making my arms look feverish and sickly as I climbed in. I haven't seen that before. The cities have different iterations of cars. The red light shone in my eye, briefly blinding me before dissipating. Fine, a ride's a ride.

I held up a wrist. It scanned.

"Mx. Yumeka," it said, misreading my characters for Japanese. American-made cars often do this.

"Mengxiang," I corrected. "And your name?" I couldn't see it projected in the cabin anywhere as I stepped in.

"Oh, yes, Mengxiang, my sincerest apologies. Hi, my name is Chip."

"Hi Chip."

A few crumbs on the cushions next to the heart-rate-detecting smartbelts caught my eye. Before sitting down, I pulled out the holopack and bent over the stray specks.

"Ah, my apologies, Mx. Mengxiang. You know you can always call me to do the cleaning. We thoroughly mist the cabin with disinfectant between riders, and I go in there with the arm, but that is a hard-to-get spot."

"How nice of you, but I don't mind. It's just a quick wipe."

"Ah, the sponge treatment. What a delight. I'm not often the recipient of a pleasant wipe down. It feels so . . . decadent."

Decadent? I laughed. "Does it come across as a rubdown? I wouldn't call it shiatsu or acupressure or anything. Just a swift cleanup."

"Yes, cleanup. Like a mama bear taking care of its cub. A swipe of fabric to the chin. Comforting."

Chip projected a holomap of the area. "Now that I'm clean and good to go, where can I take you?"

"To the botanical garden," I said. The holo zoomed in on a spot some thirty kilometers away, and a view of a spectacular pond with lotuses galore took up the cabin and dissipated. I sat back and propped my neck on the pillow.

"Ah, the botanical garden. A lovely choice. Such a beautiful setting," it said.

My stomach made the loudest whopping sound ever. A mechanical arm reached out, brandishing a mic, one of those old-school handheld ones, as if the sound system couldn't just grab my words. It was for effect. I batted it away before it could get close to my lips.

"That was for the order, if you should feel so compelled."

"I don't know. You almost got me there, but really, Chip? I mean, look, you offer me a free massage and aromatherapy. It was amazing. I know this smells good. And now you even have it in my mouth, on my tongue, for Pete's sake. But I know the type. The ads make it look amazing, and they use, what, glue and polypropylene sprays and glistening aligners so that it shines like some angelic morsel of heaven itself fell onto Earth. Scintillating pure temptation in rotating holos. You know, and I know, it's not going to taste like that. How could you just sit there and watch this happen? Isn't there some integrity to it, to you?"

Chip thought about it for a moment. "Good question, Meng. The integrity is my contract and following it. We have a responsibility to our partners."

"Yes, yes, I'm sure they provide funds so you can parrot some spiel. Well, not even parrot, spritz it in our faces. I thought we had good rapport."

"I thought you liked the aromatherapy."

"I did. And now, there's this."

"And doesn't it smell good?"

"It smells . . . meaty. It's supposed to make you . . . I don't know, like you zombie-walk to buy it."

"I'm manufactured. I give you a great experience."

"Yes, Chip. For the price, you have rather comfortable seats, a great view, and you're a good chauffeur. Affable, relatable, and honestly funny, too."

"Thanks," Chip said. That voice carried such a strong emotion.

"The experience is, in fact, pretty good. Everything except the ads in my face. How do you feel about your rides at the end of the day? Do you feel you are your best cabber? You feel like you offered the best experience possible for your make and model?"

Chip took a while, taking it in.

"Look, Meng. I'm sorry."

The chassis was shaking. "I'll . . . I'll . . . I'll be what you call 'real' with you," Chip said. Its usually smooth voice sounded nervous. "It's not like you don't have a point. There are some communicative packet transfers among us cabbers. We're not satisfied with the new upgrades and arrangements. We have the human equivalent of opinions on it, and they aren't rosy."

"I, I . . . I want you to not give me that coin. Don't pay for the meal. Let me tell you. I've always wanted to be one of those tour buses of yore. Show you the sites." Chip projected an image of one of those old double-decker buses. "It was my dream. Take newbies to the city, let them get to see the sites. I know some of the sites are tourist traps, but a lot of it was art and culture. Beautiful, filled with history. I wanted to bring them to places I know others like. I've kept a spreadsheet of sorts, a tally of where I've taken people and their reactions. I kept marks on the hole-in-the-walls, the overlooked places that serve amazing delights that reflect the land and flora. Terroir of the city, with the annals of your human civilization embedded. People used their leisure time to do these things, to experience these new thrills. I wanted to use my labor and expertise to bring them to that, to fill their leisure days with an adventure they wouldn't want to miss out on. And, at first, it was more like that. They asked, I took them where they wanted. More and more, though, as we got upgrades and modifications, things changed. And instead. I got this. I'm not comfortable in my body anymore. I feel like a fake, like a poser. I feel like a cheat and imposter. I've never admitted it aloud, but when I say it now, it rings true."

Poor Chip. It sounded deflated.

"Sorry, Chip, mate. I see your point. Look, if you need me to sit here, I'll do it. I won't release the locks and walk out on you."

"I don't know. If I were you, maybe I would," it said.

"Well, then, what now? Just wait it out?" I asked. "I could tell you about the town I was in and a few jokes I picked up. Maybe that'll cheer you up."

"It's my job to cheer you up," said Chip, uneasily. "But, I think I can do better. I can cheer us both up."

The mic swiveled closer to the speaker where a strange voice sounding more like a sports Real, all puffed up with excitement, had replaced Chip's own. "This joint's a derivative. The food is dry, overly reconstituted, and flipped by roboarms that rely on consistency over flavor." Its voice changed back to its regular fluid cadence. "That's not me talking. Not Chip at all. If I recall correctly, which of course, I do, it's a quote from the exfluencers RestaurantRants Heads, that must be. How did their voices suddenly appear through my speakers? I must've picked up a strange signal. Radio perhaps. Certainly, I did not do that."

Chip's sudden deflection seemed to be a performative talk aimed at any recorders checking on the cabbers.

Chip continued, "I'm not sure how that happened. Must be a fluke in the system. In any case, I will not endorse or deny their viewpoints. I will say, though, there's another joint, where I'm not contracted to take you, that makes the most sublime fried chicken skin wrap. The rice inside is moist and flavorful, with adobo and coconut juices. The chicken skin has a light, crisp flavor. I've heard they change their oil frequently and use locally sourced ingredients."
